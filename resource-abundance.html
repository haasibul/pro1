A country's factor endowments — its relative supplies of labor and capital — determine which goods it produces most efficiently and therefore which goods it exports.

01 — Project Overview

What Is This Page About?

This page demonstrates how differences in factor endowments — the relative supplies of labor and capital — determine trade patterns through the Heckscher-Ohlin framework.

If labor were the only factor of production — as the Ricardian model assumes — comparative advantage could only arise from international differences in labor productivity. But the real world is richer than that. Canada exports forest products to the United States not because its lumberjacks are more productive, but because sparsely populated Canada has more forested land per capita.

"That international trade is largely driven by differences in countries' resources is one of the most influential theories in international economics."

— Heckscher-Ohlin Model, International Trade Theory

The H-O model explains trade through the interaction between factor abundance and factor intensity. A country exports goods whose production uses intensively the factor that country holds in relative abundance.

What Activity Inspired This?

A spreadsheet lab where we computed L/K ratios for 10 goods, ranked them by factor intensity, and matched them to country endowments to predict trade patterns.

What Concept Is Demonstrated?

The H-O Theorem: a country abundant in labor exports labor-intensive goods; a country abundant in capital exports capital-intensive goods.

Why Does It Matter?

H-O explains global trade patterns, Stolper-Samuelson income distribution effects, China's export evolution, and why factor-price equalization is theoretically predicted but empirically elusive.

Theorem Statement

The country that is abundant in a factor exports the good whose production is intensive in that factor.

Key Terms from the Model

As used throughout the Heckscher-Ohlin model.

Factor Intensity

The relative amount of labor and capital used to produce a good — measured by the L/K ratio.

Labor-Intensive

A good using a high ratio of labor to capital (L/K > 1).

Capital-Intensive

A good using a high ratio of capital to labor (L/K < 1).

Factor Endowment

The total amounts of labor and capital a country possesses.

Labor-Abundant

A country whose L/K ratio is higher than its trading partner's — defined relatively, not absolutely.

Capital-Abundant

A country whose K/L ratio is higher — relatively more capital per worker than its trading partner.

Stolper-Samuelson

Trade raises the real return to a country's abundant factor and lowers the return to its scarce factor.

Rybczynski Effect

An increase in one factor's supply expands the good that uses it intensively, contracts the other.

Leontief Paradox

Leontief (1953) found U.S. exports were less capital-intensive than imports — paradoxical for a capital-rich nation.

04 — What Did I Learn?

Knowledge, Skills & Reflection

Reflections across three dimensions: conceptual knowledge, practical skills, and honest engagement with what was difficult, surprising, and applicable.

📚

Knowledge

  • The H-O theorem attributes trade to factor endowment differences, not just productivity differences as in Ricardo's model.
  • The L/K ratio classifies goods: labor-intensive (L/K > 1) vs. capital-intensive (L/K < 1).
  • The Stolper-Samuelson Effect: trade raises the real return to the abundant factor and permanently lowers it for the scarce factor.
  • The Rybczynski Effect: growing one factor expands the sector that uses it intensively while shrinking the other.
  • Factor-price equalization fails empirically due to technology gaps, barriers, and transport costs.
  • The Leontief Paradox: the U.S. exported labor-intensive goods — challenging the pure H-O model.
🔧

Skills

  • Computing L/K ratios using spreadsheet formulas and interpreting their economic meaning.
  • Sorting and ranking data in Excel to identify factor intensity ordering across goods.
  • Applying a two-factor, two-country theoretical model to classify real trade predictions.
  • Reading empirical data critically — recognizing when data contradicts the model.
  • Connecting microeconomic production theory to macroeconomic trade patterns.
  • Evaluating model limitations: technology differences and assumption violations.
💡

Reflection

  • Difficult: Understanding why factor-price equalization fails — technology gaps cause persistent wage differences the model ignores.
  • Surprising: The Leontief Paradox: the most capital-abundant country exported labor-intensive goods — resolves when human capital is accounted for.
  • Real-world: China's export shift toward skill-intensive sectors is a live H-O experiment — the Rybczynski Effect in action.
  • Open question: Can a modified H-O model accounting for productivity differences serve as a universal trade framework?
