The Gravity Model of Trade

Physicists use gravity to describe attraction between masses. Economists borrowed the same mathematics: trade between two countries is proportional to the product of their economic sizes and inversely proportional to the distance between them. A large economy close to another large economy trades a lot. A small economy far from everyone trades a little. This model, surprisingly simple, predicts real-world trade flows with remarkable accuracy.

What I learnedDistance is not just physical — it includes language barriers, legal differences, and cultural distance. Understanding this helped me see why a small country joining a trade bloc can punch far above its economic weight.

Ricardex — Comparative Advantage

David Ricardo proved something that still feels strange: even if Portugal is better at making both wine and cloth than England, both countries are better off if Portugal focuses on wine and England on cloth — as long as each specializes in what it gives up less to produce. The concept is comparative advantage, and it turns the intuition that "only the best should produce" completely on its head.

What I learnedAbsolute productivity is less important than relative opportunity cost. Two students in a group project should split tasks by what each gives up least — the same logic drives $25 trillion in annual global trade.

Resource Abundance & Trade Patterns

Heckscher and Ohlin extended Ricardo's logic: countries don't just differ in productivity, they differ in what they have plenty of. A country rich in labour exports things that need a lot of labour. A country with abundant capital exports capital-intensive goods. This explains why Bangladesh exports garments and Germany exports machinery — not because one is "better," but because each has a different endowment.

What I learnedFactor endowments aren't fixed forever — education, infrastructure, and capital accumulation change them. Today's labour-intensive exporter can become tomorrow's technology exporter through deliberate investment.

Systems Thinking & Economies of Scale

When you produce more, the cost per unit falls. Not just a little — sometimes dramatically. This changes everything about where production happens and why. Systems thinking reveals that these effects don't operate in isolation: lower costs attract more buyers, more buyers mean more volume, more volume means lower costs again. These feedback loops explain why certain industries cluster in a handful of countries and refuse to move.

What I learnedTrade isn't only about endowments or productivity differences. Sometimes two identical countries trade with each other simply because scale matters. Getting big enough can itself become the competitive advantage.

The Autarky Trap

Autarky means producing everything yourself and trading with nobody. It sounds like independence. In economic terms, it is a trap. When a country refuses to import, it must produce goods it is bad at making — paying higher costs and forgoing the consumption gains that specialization allows. This project maps that loss precisely and asks: how much does the security of self-sufficiency actually cost?

What I learnedNo modern economy is fully autarkic, but policy choices — tariffs, quotas, export bans — push countries along a spectrum. Every step toward autarky has a welfare cost that is calculable, even if rarely communicated to voters.

Binary Decision Optimizer

Some decisions cannot be "sort of yes." You either open the factory or you don't. You either hire the contractor or you don't. Binary integer programming handles exactly this: decision variables that can only be 0 or 1. The challenge is that with many binary variables, the number of possible combinations explodes — but the algorithm finds the profit-maximizing or cost-minimizing combination within your constraints.

What I learnedRounding a continuous LP solution to the nearest integer often gives the wrong answer — and not just slightly wrong. The proper 0-1 formulation exists because rounding shortcuts can violate constraints or miss significantly better solutions.

LP Solver

A business has limited machines, limited workers, limited time. Linear programming translates those limitations into lines on a graph. The area where all constraints are satisfied is called the feasible region. The best possible outcome always sits at a corner of that region. The simplex algorithm visits corners efficiently until it finds the one that maximizes profit or minimizes cost — that corner is the optimal solution.

What I learnedFormulating the problem is harder than solving it. Identifying which constraints are binding, which variables matter, and what the objective function actually represents — that is the real managerial skill. The algorithm is just arithmetic.

Branch and Bound

The LP solver gives you 3.7 machines. Real life gives you 3 or 4. Branch and bound closes that gap: it solves the LP, sees the fractional answer, then branches into two sub-problems — one where that variable is forced down to the nearest integer, one where it is forced up. Each branch is bounded by the best LP solution it could possibly achieve. Branches that can't beat the best integer solution found so far are pruned. The result is the true integer optimum.

What I learnedThe elegance of branch and bound is that it avoids checking every possible integer combination. Smart bounding eliminates entire subtrees of the decision space, making an exponential problem practically solvable.

Travelling Salesman Problem

A salesman must visit 10 cities exactly once and return home. How many possible routes are there? 181,440. With 20 cities: 60 quadrillion. The Travelling Salesman Problem is famously NP-hard, meaning no algorithm can guarantee the best solution in reasonable time as the number of cities grows. This project explores why, and what heuristic approaches — nearest neighbour, 2-opt improvements — allow us to find very good solutions without checking every route.

What I learnedPerfect is the enemy of good. In combinatorial problems, an algorithm that finds a 98%-optimal solution in seconds is often worth far more than one that finds 100%-optimal in days. Knowing when to use heuristics versus exact methods is itself a decision-making skill.

Workforce Planning

A hospital needs 20 nurses on Monday mornings and 8 on Sunday nights. Each nurse works five consecutive days. How many nurses should be hired and when should each start their rotation? Workforce planning formulates this as a linear program: minimize total staff cost while satisfying coverage requirements at every time period. The solution tells you exactly how many people to hire and when — not based on intuition, but on mathematics.

What I learnedThe hardest part is writing the coverage constraints correctly. A common mistake is constraining total hours rather than simultaneous presence. Getting the formulation right is everything — once correct, the solver does the rest in milliseconds.
