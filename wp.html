Smart Order Picking Strategies

A picker needs to grab 12 items scattered across the warehouse floor. Three different walking strategies, one goal: the shortest trip back to base.

01 · Background

Why Picking Routes Matter

Walking the warehouse floor to gather items is one of the slowest parts of fulfilling an order. A smarter path through the aisles can shave real time off every single pick run.

🏷️

The Setup

A picker starts at the depot, needs to grab 12 different items from their shelf spots, and must return to the depot when finished. The goal is the shortest possible walking distance.

🏬

The Floor Plan

The warehouse has 6 aisles running side by side, with storage shelves lining both walls of each aisle. Items sit at random heights along these shelves.

🚀

Why Not Just Solve It Exactly?

Checking every possible order of 12 stops would take forever to compute. Instead, simple rule-based strategies (heuristics) get close to the best answer almost instantly.

🧩

The Three Strategies

Zone Sweep clears one section of the warehouse at a time. Serpentine Path snakes through every aisle in order. Gap Skip avoids walking into empty stretches of an aisle.

Strategy 1
Zone Sweep

Split the floor into a few sections, each covering a couple of neighboring aisles. Clear out every item in the first section before moving on to the next — like cleaning one room fully before stepping into the next.

1Group the aisles into sections — left side, middle, and right side of the floor.
2Start with whichever section is closest to the depot.
3Pick up every item in that section before stepping into the next one.
4Head back to the depot once the last section is cleared.

Strategy 2
Serpentine Path

Walk straight through every aisle that has at least one item, going all the way from one end to the other. Each time you cross into a new aisle, flip direction — so the overall path looks like a zig-zag snake across the floor.

1Figure out which aisles actually have items to grab.
2Walk into the first aisle from the near end and go all the way to the far end, grabbing items along the way.
3Step across to the next aisle and walk back the opposite direction. Repeat this back-and-forth.
4Once the last aisle is finished, head straight back to the depot.

Strategy 3
Gap Skip

Before entering an aisle, check where the biggest empty stretch is — at the near end, between two items, or right at the far end. If that empty stretch is at the far end, just walk in, grab everything, and walk back out the same way instead of continuing to the end.

1Order the items in each aisle from nearest to farthest.
2Find the largest empty stretch — it could be before the first item, between two items, or after the last one.
3If that empty stretch is at the far end, walk in, collect everything, then turn around and walk back out.
4Otherwise, walk straight through, collecting up to the gap, then exit out the far side.

05 · Takeaways

What I Took Away From This

A few things stood out after building and comparing these three walking strategies.

What I Learned

Good Enough Beats Perfect (Usually)

None of these strategies are guaranteed to find the absolute shortest path — but they don't need to. Gap Skip came out ahead simply because it avoids one specific kind of wasted movement: walking into an aisle further than necessary. Small, targeted rules can beat brute-force thinking.

Skills I Practiced

Turning a Floor Plan Into Numbers

Mapping out a physical space as x/y coordinates, then measuring distance step-by-step, made it possible to actually compare strategies instead of just guessing. Breaking the whole-floor problem down into per-aisle decisions made a messy problem much easier to handle.

Reflection

The "Obvious" Choice Wasn't Best

The Serpentine Path felt like the most natural way to walk a warehouse — just go straight through everything. But it turned out to be the slowest, because it ignores how empty an aisle actually is. It's a good reminder that the simplest approach isn't always the most efficient one.
