Project Overview

"An empirical relationship known as the gravity model helps to make sense of the value of trade between any pair of countries and sheds light on the impediments that continue to limit international trade even in today's global economy."

— International Trade: Theory and Policy
What Inspired This

Chapter 2 of Krugman's International Trade: Theory and Policy introduced the gravity model as a way to predict trade volumes between countries. The textbook demonstrates that the largest European economies — Germany, UK, France — also happen to be the US's top European trade partners, suggesting a strong link between economic size and trade volume. Our class assignment applied this model to 12 country pairs, predominantly in South and Central Asia, to test how well it holds up in practice.

The Core Concept

The gravity model borrows from physics: just as gravitational attraction between two bodies depends on their masses and distance, bilateral trade between two countries scales proportionally with the product of their GDPs and inversely with the distance separating them. A scaling constant A captures everything else — trade agreements, political relations, cultural ties, and border effects — that the simple GDP-distance formula misses.

Why This Matters

The gravity model is one of the most empirically successful tools in economics. It helps governments evaluate trade agreements (do they actually increase trade beyond what GDP and distance predict?), identify geopolitical friction (India–Pakistan trades far below its gravitational "potential"), and understand why even borderless free-trade zones still see dramatic drops in cross-border commerce compared to domestic trade.

Assignment Tasks

Students were given 12 country pairs. For each pair, we: (1) computed predicted trade assuming A = 1, (2) looked up actual bilateral trade volumes via the OEC, (3) derived the implied A for each pair, (4) averaged A across all pairs, (5) re-predicted trade using the average A, (6) calculated residuals, and (7) wrote up explanations for the closest and farthest pairs from prediction.

India ↔ Pakistan Residual: −76.20
Despite geographic proximity (689 km) and large economies, actual trade is only $1.24bn vs a predicted $77.44bn. Decades of geopolitical conflict, border restrictions, visa regimes, and the absence of a free trade agreement suppress trade far below what the model expects. A = 0.0005 — the lowest in the dataset. This illustrates how political barriers can completely override economic gravity.
India ↔ Bangladesh Residual: −34.37
Predicted trade is $47.53bn but actual is only $13.16bn. Despite shared history and a land border, non-tariff barriers, currency mismatches, informal trade flows not captured in official data, and weak cross-border logistics infrastructure all dampen formal trade. Informal trade between these countries is thought to be substantial but is excluded from official bilateral figures.
Kazakhstan ↔ Uzbekistan Residual: +2.96
A = 0.108, the highest in the dataset. Despite small economies, both countries trade significantly more than the basic model predicts. Post-Soviet integration, shared infrastructure, cultural and linguistic ties, and membership in the Eurasian Economic Union all amplify trade beyond what GDP and distance alone would predict.
Russia ↔ Kazakhstan Residual: +17.18
Actual trade ($27.75bn) nearly triples the base prediction ($10.57bn). Russia and Kazakhstan share the world's longest contiguous land border, a common post-Soviet infrastructure network, use of Russian as a business language, and deep trade integration through the Eurasian Economic Union. A = 0.083 reflects these compounding favorable factors.
India ↔ Nepal Residual: +0.01
The closest pair to model prediction in the entire dataset. Nepal's small economy and short distance from India combine with an open treaty of trade and transit (the 1996 Indo-Nepal Trade Treaty). The model captures this relationship almost perfectly — actual $7.93bn vs predicted $7.92bn.
Indonesia ↔ Pakistan Residual: +0.23
The second-closest pair to the model. Despite significant distance (5,220 km), trade flows are close to expected. Both are large Muslim-majority developing economies with some historical trading links and compatible export baskets (textiles, commodities). A = 0.033 is close to the dataset average, making this pair a near-ideal representation of what "normal" looks like.

Textbook Connection

The textbook illustrates that US trade with European countries clusters around a 45° line when GDP share is plotted against trade share — a visual validation of the model. Our assignment replicates this logic for South and Central Asian pairs, finding that while the model's general direction is correct (larger GDPs → more trade), the residuals reveal enormous political and structural variation that GDP and distance alone cannot explain.

The Border Effect Parallel

Research on the Canada–US border demonstrates that even one of the world's most open borders deters trade equivalent to being 1,500–2,500 miles farther apart. Our India–Pakistan pair illustrates an extreme version of this: the political "border effect" is so large it collapses trade to near-zero despite geographic adjacency and large GDPs, lowering A to 0.0005 — roughly 63× smaller than the dataset average.

04

What Did I Learn?

📚
Knowledge
  • The gravity model predicts bilateral trade from GDP and distance, with a constant A capturing structural factors.
  • Trade is proportional to the product of GDPs — doubling both countries' GDPs would quadruple predicted trade.
  • A 1% increase in distance is associated with a 0.7–1% fall in trade, reflecting both transport costs and reduced personal contact.
  • Post-Soviet countries (Russia–Kazakhstan, Kazakhstan–Uzbekistan) show elevated A values due to legacy infrastructure and trade integration.
  • Political conflict (India–Pakistan) can suppress trade to near-zero regardless of economic size or proximity.
🔧
Skills
  • Data collection: Used the OEC to find bilateral trade values for 12 country pairs and cross-referenced with World Bank GDP data.
  • Model application: Applied the gravity formula in a spreadsheet, deriving A for each pair from observed trade data.
  • Statistical reasoning: Evaluated the trade-offs between simple arithmetic averaging vs. weighted or median approaches for estimating A.
  • Anomaly analysis: Identified and explained outliers using political economy, regional history, and trade agreement knowledge.
  • Critical thinking: Recognized the model's limits — that a single constant cannot capture the full range of real-world variation.
💡
Reflection
  • What was difficult: Finding reliable, consistent bilateral trade data. Different sources (OEC, UN Comtrade, World Bank) sometimes showed significantly different figures for the same pair, requiring judgment calls.
  • What surprised me: How dramatically geopolitics can overwhelm economic forces. India and Pakistan are closer than India and Indonesia, yet trade 24× less. The model is blind to this.
  • Real-world application: Governments use gravity models to evaluate whether trade agreements are actually working — if post-agreement trade is still below the gravity prediction, the agreement may not have removed the key barriers. This is exactly how economists assessed NAFTA and later USMCA.
  • Deeper question: The constant A is really a black box for everything we don't understand. A more sophisticated model would decompose A into separate variables for trade agreements, language, colonial history, and political relations.
Q1 — Which two pairs were closest to the model's prediction?
India–Nepal (residual = +$0.01bn) and Indonesia–Pakistan (residual = +$0.23bn) came closest. India–Nepal benefits from an open trade treaty and geographic integration; Indonesia–Pakistan's A value of 0.033 sits almost exactly at the dataset average, making it a near-textbook illustration of what "normal" gravity looks like. These pairs suggest the model performs best when no exceptional political or institutional factors distort the baseline relationship.
Q2 — Which two pairs were farthest from prediction?
India–Pakistan (residual = −$76.20bn) and India–Bangladesh (residual = −$34.37bn) deviated most dramatically. India–Pakistan's divergence stems from deep geopolitical hostility: restricted border crossings, limited diplomatic ties, and absence of a bilateral trade agreement suppress trade to a tiny fraction of what the model expects. India–Bangladesh underperforms due to non-tariff barriers, informal trade not captured in official statistics, and infrastructural bottlenecks at land border crossings.
Q3 — What does the constant A capture that GDP and distance cannot?
A is a structural multiplier for everything the basic formula ignores: trade agreements and tariff regimes (post-Soviet EEU membership inflates A for Russia–Kazakhstan), political relations (conflict deflates A for India–Pakistan to near-zero), cultural and linguistic ties (shared Soviet-era language of commerce elevates Central Asian pairs), border infrastructure (quality of roads and ports), colonial history, and diaspora networks. The gravity model, as Krugman notes, uses A to identify anomalies — the real interest is in explaining why A varies so widely across pairs.
Q4 — What's a better way to estimate the average A?
A simple arithmetic mean is distorted by extreme outliers like India–Pakistan (A = 0.0005) pulling the average down and Kazakhstan–Uzbekistan (A = 0.108) pulling it up. Better approaches: (1) Trade-weighted average — weight each pair's A by its actual trade volume, giving more influence to economically significant pairs; (2) Median — more robust to outliers and gives A = ~0.028 for our dataset; (3) Winsorized mean — cap extreme values at the 10th/90th percentile before averaging; (4) Separate A by region or political type — recognizing that a single A cannot meaningfully summarize pairs as different as India–Nepal and India–Pakistan.
