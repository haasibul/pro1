This project page is based on a class activity that introduced two important business concepts: Systems Thinking and Economies of Scale. The activity used a factory production scenario to compare two different production sizes — a small factory with one machine and a large factory with five machines — to see how increasing output changes the economics.

The key finding was simple but powerful: when the number of machines increased from 1 to 5, the cost per unit dropped by 80%, and the factory went from breaking even to earning ₩8 profit per unit. The fixed costs stayed exactly the same — only the output changed.

These two concepts are important because:

🔗

Systems Thinking

Helps us understand how parts of a business connect to each other and why changing one thing can affect everything else.

📈

Economies of Scale

Explains why bigger factories can produce cheaper — fixed costs spread across more units, so each unit costs less.

⚖️

Limits Exist

Scale advantages don't last forever. Demand, competition, and management problems eventually stop the cost from falling.

A. What Is a System?

A system is a group of connected things that work together. It has inputs (what goes in), a process (what happens inside), and outputs (what comes out). Systems also have stocks (things that build up) and flows (rates of change), and feedback loops that connect outputs back to inputs.
⬇️

Input

What enters the system — materials, energy, money, labour.

⚙️

Process

What happens inside — manufacturing, cooking, growing.

⬆️

Output

What comes out — products, profit, oxygen, heat.

📦

Stock

What builds up inside — inventory, water in a tank.

🔄

Flow

The rate at which stock changes — units per day, litres per hour.

↩️

Feedback

When outputs loop back and change future inputs.

B. How to Apply Systems Thinking

Systems thinking means studying how parts of a system relate to each other, not just the parts individually. You follow these steps:
1

Define the Boundaries

Decide what is inside and outside the system. Where does it start and end?

2

Identify the Parts

List all components, people, and elements inside the boundary.

3

Map the Relationships

Show which parts connect to each other and in what direction.

4

Find Stocks and Flows

What builds up (stock)? What changes those amounts over time (flow)?

5

Identify the Function

What is the system's overall purpose? What behaviour does it produce?

E. Can Scale Go On Forever?

No. Economies of scale have limits. After a certain point, costs stop going down and start rising again — this is called diseconomies of scale. There are also external forces that stop growth.
📉

Decreased Demand

If you produce more than the market wants, you must cut prices and profit disappears.

⚔️

Competition

When your factory is profitable, competitors enter and reduce your market share.

📜

Regulations

Government rules on labour, environment, and antitrust can add costs or limit production.

🔬

Technology Change

A new technology can make your scaled-up system obsolete overnight.

🏗️

Management Problems

Large organisations become harder to manage — more bureaucracy, slower decisions, higher internal costs.

🌍

Resource Limits

Raw materials, workers, and land become scarcer and more expensive at very high volumes.

Total OutputQ = M × U  (machines × units per machine per day)
Variable CostTVC = Q × v  (total output × variable cost per unit)
Total Daily CostTC = FC + TVC  (fixed cost stays the same no matter how much you produce)
Cost per UnitCPU = TC ÷ Q  (this falls as Q rises — that is economies of scale)
Daily RevenueR = p × Q  (price × total output)
Daily Profitπ = R − TC  (revenue minus total cost)
Margin per Unitm = p − CPU  (only positive when price is higher than cost per unit)
Break-even Pricep* = CPU  (the minimum price needed to cover all costs)

What Did I Learn?

"The biggest surprise was how dramatically the cost per unit fell when machines increased from 1 to 5. The fixed costs stayed exactly the same — only the output changed — and that one difference transformed a break-even operation into a profitable one."
🧠
Dimension 1
Knowledge
  • Systems are about relationships between parts, not just the parts themselves.
  • Fixed costs stay constant — this is what makes economies of scale work.
  • The break-even price equals cost per unit, and it changes every time volume changes.
  • A factory is a feedback loop — profit funds the next production cycle.
  • Diseconomies of scale happen when coordination costs outgrow the savings.
⚙️
Dimension 2
Skills
  • Cost modelling: applying the CPU formula to calculate total cost, revenue, and profit step by step.
  • Systems mapping: drawing the boundary, parts, stocks, flows, and feedback loops of a factory.
  • Scenario analysis: holding some inputs constant while changing others to isolate the cause.
  • Data reading: interpreting bar charts and comparison tables to find patterns.
  • Critical thinking: asking when and why scale advantages stop working.
💭
Dimension 3
Reflection

What was difficult: At first I thought all costs would go up with production. Understanding that fixed costs stay exactly the same was the key idea I had to grasp before everything made sense.

What surprised me: A 5× increase in machines caused an 80% drop in cost per unit. The same ₩1,000 cost behaved completely differently at 100 units versus 500 units.

What I want to explore: At what exact point do management costs start rising faster than the fixed-cost savings? This question seems important for real business decisions.

🌐 Real-World Connection
This factory scenario is a small version of something that shapes entire economies. Large exporters like Samsung, Hyundai, and POSCO hold cost advantages simply because they produce at much higher volumes than competitors. When one manufacturer produces five million units and another produces one hundred thousand, the fixed-cost advantage is too large to overcome through efficiency alone. In international trade theory, this connects to comparative advantage — a country's ability to produce cheaply at scale determines what it should specialise in and export. The two-scenario exercise in this class showed me the mathematical foundation behind that idea.
