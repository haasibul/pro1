01 — Overview
What is this project about?

This project came from an assignment where we used AI to generate five distinct LP problems, then selected one to solve in full. I chose a furniture manufacturing problem — a café deciding how many chairs and tables to produce each week to maximize profit.

The core concept is Linear Programming: expressing a business decision mathematically so it can be solved exactly. Every LP has three parts — decision variables (what we control), an objective function (what we optimize), and constraints (what limits us).

LP matters because it removes guessing. Instead of trying different combinations by hand, the Corner Point Theorem tells us the optimal solution always lives at a vertex of the feasible region — a finite set of points we can evaluate directly.

The Problem

A workshop produces chairs (x₁) and tables (x₂). Available weekly: 120 carpentry hours, 80 finishing hours. Each chair needs 2 carpentry + 1 finishing hour; each table needs 4 + 3. Retailer caps: max 30 chairs and 20 tables per week. Profit is ₩15,000/chair and ₩35,000/table. What production mix maximizes weekly profit?

Five-Step Modeling Process

1
Understand the Problem
Identify what decision is being made. Here: how many chairs and tables to produce per week.
2
Identify Sets, Parameters, Variables
Sets: 𝒫={chairs,tables}, ℒ={carpentry,finishing}. Parameters: C_p, A_{p,l}, B_l, D_p. Variables: x_p ≥ 0.
3
Write the Objective Function
max Z = 15000x₁ + 35000x₂ — a weighted sum of profits across all products.
4
Define All Constraints
Labor capacity: Σ A_{p,l}x_p ≤ B_l. Demand caps: x_p ≤ D_p. Non-negativity.
5
Assemble & Verify
Combine into standard form. Confirm every term is linear — no squared variables, no products of variables.

04 — What Did I Learn?
Knowledge, Skills & Reflection
Knowledge

What I Learned

  • Formal LP notation: calligraphic sets, uppercase parameters, lowercase variables
  • Every LP has an objective, functional constraints, and variable-type constraints
  • The Corner Point Theorem: the optimal always lies at a vertex of the feasible region
  • Binding constraints define which edges meet at the optimal corner
  • Continuous variables allow fractional solutions — different from integer programs
Skills

What I Practiced

  • Translating a word problem into a precise mathematical model
  • Identifying which constraints are active vs. slack at the solution
  • Using Google Sheets Solver to verify hand-calculated results
  • Prompting AI to generate realistic, varied optimization problems
  • Evaluating multiple candidate solutions for feasibility
Reflection

What Surprised Me

The hardest part was writing the constraints. The objective function felt natural, but real problems have hidden limits — like demand caps — that are easy to miss.

I was surprised that point D has three binding constraints simultaneously (C1, C2, C4) even though only two can define a 2D vertex. The demand cap x₂=20 happens to pass exactly through the C1∩C2 intersection — a coincidence in this data.

In the real world, manufacturers use exactly this structure for weekly production planning. Toyota runs LP solvers every shift to balance trim levels against paint booth and assembly line capacity.
