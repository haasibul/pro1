Project Overview
🌐
What Is This Page About?
Assignment context · Core concept · Why it matters

This project was built as part of an International Trade Economics course assignment exploring the foundations of why nations trade. The assignment asked us to analyze two countries producing two goods and determine, step by step, who holds absolute advantage, how to construct an opportunity cost matrix, and which country should specialize in which good according to Comparative Advantage — David Ricardo's central insight from 1817.

Rather than submitting a static worksheet, I built an interactive tool that replicates the full analytical workflow. Any two countries and any two goods can be entered, and the tool computes every result instantly — from raw productivity comparisons through to PPF configurations and world output gains from specialization.

Course Assignment
International Trade Economics — Comparative Advantage Analysis
⚡
Absolute Advantage
A country has absolute advantage when it can produce more of a good per resource unit than another. Introduced by Adam Smith. Important, but not the full story.
⚖️
Opportunity Cost
The real cost of producing one unit of a good is the quantity of the other good that must be given up. Calculated as: OC(Good 1) = output(Good 2) ÷ output(Good 1).
🎯
Comparative Advantage
Ricardo's breakthrough insight: Trade is mutually beneficial if countries specialize in goods where they hold a lower opportunity cost, even if they lack absolute advantage in both.

Mathematical Formula Progression
1
Westland Opp. Cost of 1 Computer = 5 Grain ÷ 10 Computers = 0.5 Grain.
2
Eastland Opp. Cost of 1 Computer = 4 Grain ÷ 2 Computers = 2.0 Grain.
3
Compare: 0.5 < 2.0. Therefore, Westland holds the Comparative Advantage in Good 1.
Notice that in the "Total Dominance" scenario, one country is completely superior in raw hour-by-hour production of both commodities. Yet, because their relative cost cross-ratios remain mathematically divergent, both systems still secure system-wide trade expansions by trading along specialization paths.
